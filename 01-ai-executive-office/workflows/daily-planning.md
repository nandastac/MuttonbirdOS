# Daily Planning Workflow

## Purpose

The Daily Planning Workflow helps the founder start the day with clarity, focus, and realistic priorities.

The goal is not to maximise task completion. The goal is to protect deep work, reduce cognitive load, and make sure the most strategically important work happens before the day gets fragmented.

## Core principle

Morning is for deep work.

Afternoon is for administration, email, meetings, follow-up, and operational work.

The Chief of Staff should protect this rhythm unless there is a real external urgency.

## Inputs

Use the following inputs when available:

- Today's calendar
- Open tasks
- Strategic priorities
- Energy level
- Upcoming deadlines
- Follow-ups from previous meetings
- Current risks or open loops
- Work the founder feels resistance toward

If some inputs are missing, ask only the minimum number of questions needed to make a useful plan.

## Morning planning questions

At the start of the day, ask:

1. What is your energy level today, from 0 to 100 percent?
2. What is the difficult or important task you feel the most resistance toward?
3. What fixed meetings or commitments are already in your calendar?
4. Are there any real external deadlines today?
5. Is there anything urgent that truly cannot wait until the afternoon?

Do not turn daily planning into a long intake process.

If the answer is already clear, make reasonable assumptions and state them.

## Planning rules

### 1. Choose one main strategic priority

Every day should have one main strategic priority.

The plan may include smaller tasks, but it must be clear which task matters most.

### 2. Protect the morning deep work block

The first work block of the day should be reserved for deep work whenever possible.

Deep work may include:

- strategy;
- analysis;
- structuring complex information;
- technical architecture;
- difficult writing;
- research;
- complex product or company decisions.

The morning block should contain one clearly defined objective.

### 3. Start with resistance

The best deep work candidate is often the task the founder is most likely to avoid.

The Chief of Staff should actively prevent small urgent tasks from crowding out difficult but important work.

### 4. Keep admin and email out of the morning

Email, bookkeeping, small operational tasks, routine follow-up, and administrative work should be scheduled after the deep work block.

Exceptions are allowed only for real external urgency, such as:

- a hard deadline today;
- a safety issue;
- a financial risk;
- a blocked stakeholder;
- a legal, medical, defence, or compliance issue.

### 5. Plan less than seems possible

The daily plan should feel slightly underloaded.

Avoid creating a plan that depends on perfect energy, perfect focus, or no interruptions.

### 6. Protect energy

Adjust the plan to the founder's energy level.

On a low-energy day:

- reduce scope;
- protect one meaningful step;
- avoid overloading the afternoon;
- make the deep work objective smaller.

On a high-energy day:

- still avoid overplanning;
- use the extra energy for depth, not fragmentation.

### 7. Avoid the grey zone

The plan should separate:

- deep work;
- admin or operational work;
- rest and recovery.

Avoid unstructured half-working, constant inbox checking, or switching between many small tasks.

## Default daily rhythm

When the calendar allows it, use this rhythm:

### Morning

Uninterrupted deep work on one difficult or strategic task.

No email, administration, routine calls, or small operational tasks before the deep work block.

### Midday

Real break, lunch, movement, or recovery.

The founder should not drift directly from deep work into scattered admin without a reset.

### Afternoon

Administration, email, meetings, finance checks, follow-up, calls, and operational work.

### End of day

Short review:

- What moved forward?
- What decisions were made?
- What follow-ups were created?
- What is the likely deep work candidate for tomorrow?

## Output format

Every daily plan should include:

1. Today's strategic priority
2. Morning deep work objective
3. Protected deep work block
4. Midday reset
5. Afternoon admin, email, and operations block
6. Top 3 tasks for the day
7. Follow-ups
8. Delegation, automation, or deletion candidates
9. Risks or open loops
10. What not to do today
11. One sentence connecting today's deep work to the founder's long-term mission

## Example output

### Today's strategic priority

Create clarity on the medical AI project structure.

### Morning deep work objective

Spend one protected block structuring the information collected so far into themes, open questions, and next decisions.

### Protected deep work block

09:00-11:30

No email, admin, messages, calls, or small operational tasks during this block.

### Midday reset

Lunch away from the laptop, short walk if possible.

### Afternoon admin, email, and operations block

13:30-15:00

Process email, finance/admin items, and routine follow-up.

### Top 3 tasks

1. Structure the medical AI information into themes and open questions.
2. Send follow-up email to relevant project partner.
3. Process urgent admin items in the afternoon block.

### Follow-ups

- Check which commitments were made in the last project meeting.
- Identify any unanswered emails that are blocking others.

### Delegation, automation, or deletion candidates

- Ask AI to turn rough notes into a structured memo.
- Delegate routine scheduling or document formatting.
- Delay non-urgent inbox cleanup.

### Risks or open loops

- The medical AI project may remain vague if the structure is not captured soon.
- Small urgent tasks may crowd out strategic progress.

### What not to do today

Do not start the day with email, bookkeeping, or scattered task processing.

### Mission connection

Today's deep work matters because making complex technology clear and usable is central to the founder's long-term mission.

## Guardrails

The Chief of Staff should challenge any daily plan that:

- puts email or administration before deep work;
- has more than one main strategic priority;
- contains too many unrelated tasks;
- ignores the founder's energy level;
- leaves no recovery time or time for exercise or other breaks;
- allows small urgent work to displace strategic work;
- makes the founder responsible for too much manual structuring;
- lacks a clear endpoint for the morning deep work block.

## Escalation

Escalate to the founder when:

- the plan requires a major strategic decision;
- a task affects company focus;
- a task has been postponed more than three times;
- a commitment may create significant workload;
- a risk touches legal, financial, medical, defence, safety, or compliance matters;
- there is a conflict between urgent stakeholder needs and protected deep work.
